./ Case Studies / ProCraftr

Overview

Problem

Create an app to allow users to manage complicated crafting projects, by organizing and tracking the various materials, references, and planning that such projects require.

Goal

My goal was to design a system that would be flexible enough to work across crafting disciplines, while maintaining clear visibility and an efficient and easy to navigate architecture, in order to minimize the user’s mental load to free them up to concentrate on the fun parts of crafting.

Tools

Figma, Zoom, Google Docs, Invision, Google Sheets, Trello

Methods

Comparative Analysis, Feature Cards, Directed Storytelling, Speed Dating, Kano Model, User Journey Map, Wireframes, Interactive Prototype, Usability Testing

Context

MANAGING COMPLICATED PROJECTS

Crafting can be a complicated space to work in - there are many parts of a project that crafters need to keep track of. Whether they’re a hobbyist or make their living selling their work, crafters need to track materials, expenses, references, images, deadlines, events, and plan out their process.


CREATE AN EFFICIENT HUB

I aimed to create an app that could act as an organizational hub for the crafting process. It should simplify a complicated space into an efficient architecture that would also be flexible enough to accommodate a variety of crafting disciplines - knitting, embroidery, sewing, jewelry, costuming, leather work - whatever craft someone might bring to the table.

Users

Crafters

The primary user for this app is a hobbyist crafter. My user research in the discovery phase pointed to problems with keeping track of items that were already owned, leading to buying extra, or needing to make another trip for something forgotten. Most of the crafters I interviewed were cosplayers - people who recreate costumes from various media. They needed to keep track of a large number of reference images, and easily lost track of files across the various places they would find or store them.


A VARIETY OF CRAFTING DISCIPLINES

Some crafters specialize in one discipline, but many dabble in at least a couple. Every test participant I interviewed worked with at least three different types of crafts. It was therefore important to me to emphasize flexibility in my design, so that users could have one tool that could work across their disciplines, rather than needing to resort to a separate app for each one.

Process

DISCOVERY RESEARCH + COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

I began by investigating currently available project management apps and crafting apps in a comparative analysis in order to evaluate existing solutions, and gain insight into common and uncommon features. My scope included systems that were not geared towards crafts, apps that targeted specific craft disciplines (quilting, knitting), and a couple of apps specifically targeting cosplayers. I also included a printable PDF I found on a crafting blog.


LOW FIDELITY WIREFRAMES + FEATURE CARDS

Using my findings from the comparative analysis, I moved into creating low fidelity wireframes to externalize burgeoning design ideas. I made these into a set of feature cards to use in my first round of user interviews, to get some early insight into specific user needs.


DIRECTED STORYTELLING AND A HYBRID SPEED DATING KANO MODEL

My first round of user research were interviews with four participants, with each session lasting about 45 - 60 minutes. I began with some directed storytelling and sensing questions. The second part of each session of was a combination of speed dating with feature cards, and questions using the Kano model. After showing the user a feature card, I asked for their initial impression of the feature, and then asked the two standard Kano model questions - how would you feel if this feature was present, and how would you feel if this feature was note present? I used the responses from the first question to validate or refine designs, and the data from the Kano questions to prioritize which areas of the app to develop first.


PERSONA, USER JOURNEY MAP, AND SCREEN FLOW

Having now better familiarized myself with my target users, I created a persona card representing the primary users, and a user journey map. The user journey map included multiple touch points ranging from initiating user awareness, to the user sharing their finished project. With a clearer vision of the user and the user’s journey, I moved to creating the architecture strategy for the app. My goal as I designed was to keep the interface clean and simple with uncluttered screens, while balancing a desire to keep the number of clicks to any part of the app to a minimum.


HIGH FIDELITY WIREFRAMES AND ACCESSIBILITY

I recreated the architecture diagram in Figma with empty frames, and then began filling in the frames with my low fidelity wireframes from the feature cards. I adjusted these as needed, and then began increasing the fidelity. I created the visual design with accessibility in mind - large buttons that a busy person with possibly sore or messy fingers could easily press without accidentally selecting something else. I was also careful to mind contrast ratios for the visually impaired, and checked the hues across various types of color blindness - one of my test participants specifically mentioned her color blind friends, and I wanted to make sure they would find the app as easy to use as anyone else.


Evaluating the High-Fidelity Prototype

After hooking up all of the wireframes into an interactive prototype in Figma, I conducted three usability test sessions with target users. I directed the users through a series of tasks leading them along a happy path through the app, focusing on the “Projects” tab and its subsequent pages.

Outcome

Usability Test Results

The usability test results were largely positive. Users were able to easily complete the tasks, and found navigation intuitive.


STRUGGLE WITH MICROCOPY

The one notable exception is some struggle with the title for the “Elements” section. I tested a few different words, and none quite fit the bill. 2/3 test participants weren’t immediately sure what that section meant, but all were able to eventually figure it out.


Next Steps

There’s a good chunk of the app left to wireframe and add to the prototype. The most pressing is to integrate the shopping list functions into the material list page, and then to flesh out the remaining screens from the architecture diagram and and some in-between screens and things like the settings screen. Once the app fully functions as a tool for an individual, I would like to add framework for community and externalization - exporting project information to a printable PDF, and sharing progress to social media.
