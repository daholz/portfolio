./ Case Studies / St Thomas More Catholic School

Overview

Problem

The client's Student Information System (SIS) is difficult for their staff to use - teachers avoid using it frequently, which leads to further difficultieswhen it comes time to turn in final grades.

Goal

Increase usability of the client’s Student Information System though improved learnability, hierarchy, and discoverability, in order to increase staff's efficient use of the program and their time, particularly during busy periods such as taking attendance or turning in end-of-period final grades.

Tools

Sketch, Invision, Google Sheets, Google Doc, Trello

Methods

Cognitive Walkthrough, Contextual Inquiry, Low Fidelity Wireframes, User Flow, High Fidelity Wireframes, Interactive Prototype Tour

" The teachers at Saint Thomas More Catholic School want to spend as much time interacting with the students as possible. "

Context

RESORTING TO NOTEBOOKS

The school’s SIS was hard for staff to use - difficult to navigate, difficult to learn, and difficult to remember how to use. Teachers often preferred to keep track of grades in analogue notebooks to minimize interactions with the SIS.


CRUNCH TIME STRESS

Submitting final grades at the end of the semester became a huge ordeal, creating and submitting grades for numerous assignments in a marathon. Many teachers turn to one teacher who’s considered the SME on the SIS for help understanding how to submit final grades, bogging down his workload even more.

Users

K - 8 TEACHERS

Teachers from Kindergarten to 8th Grade need to use the SIS daily to log attendance during the hustle and bustle of the morning routine. They also need to record assignments, grades, track patterns in student attendance, and submit final grades.


OFFICE ADMINISTRATORS

Office administrators use the system to record tardy and absent students, and to follow up with teachers’ attendance records in order to call the families of missing students. They also use the SIS at the end of the semester to create report cards that go out to the students and their families.

Role

TEAM RESEARCH

I worked as part of a team to conduct user research interviews. We developed our script and plan as a team. I moderated one remote contextual interview, and took notes as an observer during the other sessions.


SOLO TASKS

I worked alone to do a cognitive walkthrough during the research phase. After the user interviews and discussing results as a group, I worked alone to ideate solutions, make low and high fidelity wireframes and storyboards, and create an interactive prototype tour.


FINAL DELIVERABLES

We did a cognitive walkthrough to quickly familiarize ourselves with the SIS, and see how the platform looks to a new user. We conducted contextual inquiries to broaden our understanding and get an regular user's perspective. Ultimately I created an interactive prototype tour to give the client a taste of what implemented solutions could look like in context, and explain some of the design reasoning.

Process

COGNITIVE WALKTHROUGH

The first step in analyzing St Thomas More’s SIS was doing a cognitive walkthrough. We had limited access to the system itself, so we used videos (sans sounds/narration) and screenshots provided by the school for this purpose. The cognitive walkthrough centered on the five most common tasks completed by the staff on the SIS platform. Our four main parameters for judging the platform were how it measures up to mental models, visibility/hierarchy, consistency/mapping, and feedback.

After conducting the cognitive walkthrough, I developed a hypothesis that the users we would be interviewing will have had issues with learnability, discoverability, and easily navigating pages due to poor hierarchy.


REMOTE CONTEXTUAL INQUIRIES

We had the opportunity to direct 45 minute to 1 hour long contextual inquiry sessions with four staff members who were familiar with the platform - one administrator, and three teachers. I moderated one session, and observed the other three. Due to the ongoing Covid-19 pandemic, we conducted these contextual inquiries remotely.

These sessions confirmed my hypothesis - the staff had no formal training using the program, and had to rely on the program’s own learnability and questioning their peers. As a result, there were quite a few things that no one, or only a few people, knew - for example, what all of the abbreviations used in the attendance spreadsheet stood for.

It also helped to highlight the priorities of the staff, what their environments were like during attendance-taking, work-arounds they developed to make the system work for them, and specific pain points. For example, the person I interviewed acted as SME for the platform, and he was often pulled away to help answer questions at the end of the semester when he was busy finalizing his own grades and helping the office get the report cards in order.


IDEATING AND PROTOTYPING

I synthesized the findings from my cognitive walkthrough and the contextual inquiry sessions into a research findings & recommendations report. The highest priority change needed to be a way to bridge that learnability gap. Given a limited ability to change the platform itself, devising a quick and easy to access training resource seemed like the best route.

A couple of the research sessions revealed that the platform had walkthrough videos, and scheduled training sessions that the teachers didn’t have the time to leverage. All three of the teachers also supplemented the platform with their own handwritten notes - either tracking grades in physical notebooks, or printing out sheets to write on. I therefore opted to make a five sheet paper user guide, with a PDF version that could be printed if the original copy was lost.

I also made a prototype of the teachers’ dashboard page with easy, feasible changes: reorganizing the widgets, updating the icons to be more indicative of their purpose, and increasing the size of the text and icons for accessibility.

Outcome

NEXT STEPS

I would have loved to have more time to work on this project. I initially planned to redesign the gradebook and attendance pages, but there simply wasn’t enough time.

I prioritized the paper guide as a quick, cheap, and easy to implement solution over redesigning the software. If I could continue this project, I would address the hierarchy and visual design of those pages first.
