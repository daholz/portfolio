Overview

Problem

New users experience drop-off and decreased activity in the first few weeks of using Foodsby. Current onboarding is not sufficiently preparing new users for how to use the service.

Goal

Teach new users about unusual Foodsby features, such as order cut-off times, rotating available restaurants, limited restaurant order capacity, and picking up delivered food at a Foodsby kiosk. Decrease time between first and second orders, increase frequency of ordering.

Tools

Invision, Sketch, Miro, Keynote, Google Survey, Google Sheets

Methods

Stakeholder Interview, Competitor Analysis, User Journey Map, Feature Cards, Developer Consult, Dot Voting, Kano Method, Annotated Wireframes

" Contactless meal delivery made easy. "

— Foodsby Home Page

Context

MENTAL MODEL DISCONNECT FOR NEW USERS

Foodsby noticed new users were taking a long time to place a second order, and ordered infrequently until the value of the service would sink in. They were often confused by having to order before a cut-off time - well before the projected delivery time, the rotating restaurant options, and having to pick up items at the shared kiosk.


FROM CONFUSED TO LOYAL

Foodsby has many loyal, long-time users that appreciate the service’s format and value. The question is, how to move users from “new” to “loyal, frequent customer” more quickly and seamlessly.


DESIGNING FOR A DEVELOPMENT BUDGET

We were given a development time budget of 4 - 5 weeks. I chose which features to development by balancing user feedback, feature complexity and estimated time cost, and maximizing impact. In the end I chose four features to recommend to the client for their current onboarding expansion, and two larger projects as potential next steps.

Users

OFFICE WORKERS WITH SHORT LUNCH HOURS

The primary user of Foodsby is a busy office worker in a large building or complex. They don’t have much time to travel, wait for food to be made, eat, and then get back to work.


REMOTE WORKERS NEED REMOTE DELIVERY

As workers navigate isolation during the pandemic, Foodsby has been looking into ways to continue serving their users as they work from home.

Role

Part team member

I worked as a part of a large team during the feature ideation phase, creating 5 of many feature cards. We consulted with a team of Foodsby developers about the timeline for creating each feature, then dot voted on eleven features to include in a Kano Method survey. I analyzed the data from this survey in a smaller group.


Part solo

After analyzing the user research results, I worked independently to choose a set of features to flesh out as high fidelity annotated wireframes. I created a feature proposal including these annotated wireframes, and the user journey map that I made.

" We explored how Foodsby compared to competitors to gain insight into the existing context and mental models users may have encountered. "

Process

RESEARCH: COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS AND USER JOURNEY MAP

I began by investigating currently available project management apps and crafting apps in a comparative analysis in order to evaluate existing solutions, and gain insight into common and uncommon features. My scope included systems that were not geared towards crafts, apps that targeted specific craft disciplines (quilting, knitting), and a couple of apps specifically targeting cosplayers. I also included a printable PDF I found on a crafting blog.


INITIAL CONCEPT DESIGN: FEATURE CARDS AND DOT VOTING

We followed this up with brainstorming possible features that could bridge opportunity gaps, and prototyping them as low-fidelity wireframes. These were made into feature cards. I created five feature cards out of the few dozen we presented to a small team of Foodsby developers to gain insight into an estimated timeframe for implementation for each feature.


USER RESEARCH: KANO METHOD

As a group, we used dot voting to select eleven features we wanted to present to users to gain further insight into their desirability using the Kano method. Foodsby emailed our survey to 800 new users (signed up for less than 2 months). We had 10 responses, which we analyzed with both discrete and continuous methods. I drew a graph with the results (above) to clearly visualize which results were the most important and desirable to new users. Cross referencing this with the development teams’ estimated time costs, I chose four features to expand into annotated high fidelity wireframes and recommend to the Foodsby team in a feature proposal.

Outcome

PROVIDE POSITIVE ALTERNATIVES, INCENTIVIZE IMMEDIATE ACTION

Of the eleven features we included in the survey, I chose two features that were in the top three “most important” results from the Kano analysis — a bar to represent how close the restaurant is to selling out for the day, and a 5% discount on next-day orders. I combined this second feature with one of my feature cards - a prompt to see tomorrow’s menus once it’s too late to order for today.

A common pain point for new users is that they’re used to ordering food right when they want it, instead of needing to plan ahead. This gives them a positive action to take when they make a mistake, and incentivizes them to act sooner rather than later.


CONSTRUCT A NEW MENTAL MODEL, ACCOMMODATE ERROR FORGIVENESS

To more directly address onboarding, I also decided to include a slideshow that is shown to the user when they log in for the first time. With just three steps, it quickly covers the aspects of the service new users are least likely to have an accurate mental model for.
