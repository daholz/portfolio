Overview

Problem

Evaluate the TalkAboutDepression.org website's navigation, way-finding, ability to provide information and advice about depression, and ensure it can be used to reduce the stigma around depression by providing a space to read and share personal stories.

Goal

Destigmatize talking about depression by providing learning resources and first-hand accounts of experiencing depression, in order to help yourself or friends who are experiencing depression or suicidal thoughts.

Tools

Sketch, Google Docs, Google Sheets, Zoom, Keynote, Trello

Methods

Heuristic Analysis, Participant Observation, High Fidelity Wireframes

Context

EVALUATE THE EXISTING SITE

The stakeholders at TalkAboutDepression (TAD) asked us to evaluate their current website’s effectiveness at achieving their goal of destigmatizing talking about depression.


RECOMMEND CHANGES

Our final deliverable for this project would be a research findings and recommendations report, highlighting problem areas and recommending a prioritized list of changes to improve their website.

Users

PEOPLE GOING THROUGH IT

TAD has two groups of primary users. The first are people with depression seeking help and looking to share and read stories about depression, with the aim of feeling less alone in their struggle.


FRIENDS OF PEOPLE GOING THROUGH IT

The second primary user group are friends and family of people experiencing mental health problems or crises. They’re looking for resources to learn about depression, and how to help their loved ones.

Remote research session

Role

INDEPENDANT RESEARCH AND REPORT

I worked with a small group, but we began by seperately conducting heuristics analysis of the TAD website. I created a heuristic analysis report to bring back to the group. I also worked alone to create a high fidelity wireframe showing recommended changes, and a final deliverable research findings and recommendations report.


GROUP RESEARCH

My team worked together to create a participant observation script, and to run the usability tests. We took turns filling the different roles. I took one turn as a moderator, several turns as the greeter and tech support, and one turn as a principle observer.

Process

HEURISTIC ANALYSIS

Each person on my team developed their own heuristic analysis report to share with the group. I chose to use Susan Weinschenk and Dean Barker’s 20 Usability Heuristics to examine the website’s usability. There were many issues that came up, but my hypothesis was that the largest issues were with simplicity, predictability, linguistic clarity, and fulfillment. I predicted that these areas would affect not only the site’s utility to users, but also the ability for the organization to achieve its goals. Going into writing the script for the usability tests as a group, those were areas that I and my team particularly wanted to probe.


PARTICIPANT OBSERVATION

Due to the Covid-19 pandemic, most of our participant observation usability tests took place remotely using Zoom. Five the tests were managed as a group, generally with one moderator, a host/tech specialist, and two observers/note takers.

To get a sense of the users’ familiarity and experience with the topic, we began each test with some delicate probing contextual questions about depression and mental health resources. We made sure to warn our participants about the sensitive content at the top of the session, and assured them they could take a break or end the test at any point - one participant did choose to end their session early.

The remaining participants were led through three scenarios exploring the client’s top goals for their website.

  • Provide quick and easy access to support tools for those who have friends experiencing a crisis
  • Provide easy access to reputable information on depression and suicide
  • Facilitate opportunities for ‘social empathy engagement’ or ways in which individuals can learn about others’ or share their own mental health journey

Outcome

SPEAK WITH A CLEAR VOICE AND PROVIDE ACTIONS

Our evaluation dug up a number of trouble areas that needed attention, but two larger themes stuck out. The organization’s voice wavered in its execution, and while there was plenty of information from reliable sources, it lacked quick actions and advice for worried users. This led to vaguely worded menus and confusing navigation, images that weren’t striking the right tone, and unfulfilled or frustrated users. However, we also found that participants generally responded well to the website’s goals, sources, color scheme, and visual hierarchy.


HIGHLIGHTING FOUR AREAS OF CONCERN

I chose to highlight four particular areas of concern, and my recommendations for addressing them: Navigation, Getting Help, Readability, and Sharing Stories. I correlated these themes to the client’s top goals, and the test participants’ difficulties with navigation, and their frustrations with the kinds of information they were finding vs the kinds of the information they hoped to find. There was a fair amount of natural overlap between the user and client goals for this project. The stated goal of the website, to de-stigmatize talking about depression and sharing reliable information about depression and suicide prevention, resonated with our test users and made it easy to align their needs with the clients to come up with solutions to suit both.
