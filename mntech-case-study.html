Overview

Problem

MnTech is rebranding with a new focus and energy. They are adding a focus on communities of interest, and need a multi-touchpoint strategy to encourage participation. They've also discovered that most eligible members don't know that they're members.

Goal

Boost awareness of membership status and benefits. Increase membership activity in communities of interest (taking place primarily on their newest touchpoint, mobilize.io). Encourage sustained engagement in communities of interest..

Tools

Google Docs, Google Sheets, Sketch, Invision

Methods

Stakeholder Interview, Deep Dive, Touchpoint User Journey Map, Storyboard, Low and High Fidelity Wireframes, Findings and Strategy Presentation

" New name, new focus, new energy. "

— Stakeholder

Context

UNINFORMED USERS

Companies sign up and pay for MnTech membership for their tech department employees. Unfortunately, not all of these eligible members are aware that they’re members, much less aware of the benefits that entails.


NEW OFFERINGS

MnTech wants to focus on creating a self-sustaining community with active members and continued participation after capturing more users - people who would sign up for the COIs, but also talk on the mobilize board, share polls and files, and show up to meet-ups.

" We focused on MnTech's new communities of interest expansion, narrowing our scope to those areas of their website, membership onboarding, social media, newsletters, and mobilize.io platform. "

Users

MEMBERS THAT DON’T KNOW THAT THEY’RE MEMBERS

Many potential users for MnTech are employees of member companies, going about their daily lives, working their jobs in tech, completely unaware that they have a community and numerous benefits available to them.


ACTIVE MEMBERS

MnTech users are people working in technology, all across Minnesota. Active users are people that want to further their careers and improve their skills, and to collaborate and share exciting developments in tech.

Role

PART OF A SMALL TEAM

I worked with a small team of UX designers in this role. We worked together to develop the overall multi-touchpoint strategy by splitting up the project into different sections of the user journey, and collaborating together to maintain consistency.


INDEPENDENT WORK

I was responsible for developing the strategy to created sustained activity and participation. I focused on the mobilize.io platform and email touchpoints for my deep dive research and wireframes. I also created and narrated that portion of our presentation, in addition to the Persona slide.

" They launched a new touchpoint attached to their brand - communities of interest - and were disappointed by the lack of participation. Looking into it, they found out that most of their members didn’t know they were members. "

Process

IDENTIFYING TOUCHPOINTS AND A DEEP DIVE

We began the project by talking with the initiator of MnTech’s communities of interest. She gave us an hour of her time to go over MnTech’s goals, values, history, and hopes. She also explained that they want to focus on creating a self-sustaining community with active members and continued participation after capturing more users - people who would sign up for the COIs, but also talk on the mobilize board, share polls and files, and show up to meet-ups.

After the interview, my group divvied up MnTech’s various touchpoints, competitors, and partners among ourselves and did a deep dive into the context of the problem. I focused on mobilize, and how we could use it to encourage an active community. I was given access to what they had set up in mobilize so far, and I scoured the forward-facing mobilize site for how-to videos. I found that mobilize had limited visual customization, but had a robust communication system using public posts, private chats, email blasts, and a communal calendar.


DISTILLING THE USER’S JOURNEY

After debriefing as a group on MnTech’s resources and competition, we moved to creating an ideal user journey map to lay out our holistic strategy. Our main target user for this strategy was an employee at a member company, someone who is already a member of MnTech but doesn’t know it yet. Less importantly, but still pertinent, this would be a person under 45, an under-represented group in MnTech’s communities.

With these characteristics in mind, we created a user journey map starting from discovery and interest, leading into information gathering, joining a COI, and ultimately entering a cycle of participation and reward. This reward could consist of knowledge, contacts, or recognition.


FORMULATING A PLAN

We divided responsibilities by stages in the user’s journey. I was responsible for the strategy involving the COI virtual platform, and focused on how to keep users coming back for more, and becoming active participants in their communities. Healthy, productive communities are more likely to encourage active members, so I turned to Google’s research into what makes a good group dynamic. Using emails and newsletters, MnTech can encourage open communication and establish common communal norms. These newsletters can also be vehicles for news about upcoming events, community polls, member recognition and spotlights, and conversation starters. By including a recap of the month’s meetings, members that weren’t able to attend can also feel included.

Recognition can work as a reward and an incentive to perform certain tasks. Creating a framework for titles and roles benefits COIs twofold. Titles attached to roles - a moderator to manage intracommunity relations, a welcomer to make new members feel welcome, a secretary to take notes for the newsletters - not only give members a sense of ownership in their community, they also take work off of the stakeholder’s overloaded plate and encourage a self-sustaining community. Titles based on seniority - ie, number of meetings attended, weeks actively participating online, etc - encourage members to continue participating regularly, and signal to newer members who has the most experience in the community.

Also, by creating a title as a badge of honor, users may be encouraged to share their involvement with the COIs on their LinkedIn profiles, thereby creating another avenue of attracting new members.

Outcome

STRATEGY PRESENTATION

Our final and largest deliverable for MnTech was a video keynote presentation explaining our findings and strategy. We centered the approximately 7 minute long presentation on the experiences of a single representative user, following a “happy path” from coming across MnTech on social media through to becoming a long-term active member of a COI. We alternated short animated scenes of the user’s journey with quick explanations of the details and reasoning of each stage of our strategy, paired with screenshots of our prototypes.


CONCLUSIONS

We identified opportunity gaps in MnTech’s website, membership onboarding, social media presence, and plan for utilizing their newest touchpoint, mobilize. We recommended a strategy that would involve:

  • reorganizing their website to create easier navigation and discoverability
  • providing company points of contact with membership welcome packets for their employees
  • increasing their presence on current social media and expanding to youtube
  • utilizing newsletter templates to maintain contact with COI participants and encourage and inspire communication on the mobilize platform
  • incentives for active and sustained participation, and taking on leadership roles within the COIs

Next Steps

There’s a good chunk of the app left to wireframe and add to the prototype. The most pressing is to integrate the shopping list functions into the material list page, and then to flesh out the remaining screens from the architecture diagram and and some in-between screens and things like the settings screen. Once the app fully functions as a tool for an individual, I would like to add framework for community and externalization - exporting project information to a printable PDF, and sharing progress to social media.
