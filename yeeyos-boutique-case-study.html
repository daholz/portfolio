./ Case Studies / Yeeyo's Boutique

Overview

Problem

Yeeyo's Boutique has a physical store, but no online presence and no branding. With the Covid-19 Pandemic making in person interaction dangerous, my client needed a strategy for expanding their enterprise online.

Goal

Create an ecommerce and contactless pickup strategy that works for a business with minimal staff (1 - 3 people), design branding and a logo.

Tools

Figma, Invision, Trello, Google Docs, Google Sheets, Miro

Methods

Competitive analysis, contextual interview, directed storytelling, feature cards, wireframes, personas, storyboards, journey map, site map, interactive prototype, usability tests

" Yeeyo’s Boutique is a small, established retail business focused on filling the needs of Muslim and East African communities who have trouble finding Somali and Islamic goods. "

- Yeeyo's Boutique Stakeholder

Context

E-COMMERCE AND ONLINE PRESENCE

With Covid-19 creating a retail environment defined by danger and uncertainty, Yeeyo’s Boutique needed to move up their plans to expand online. They wanted an e-commerce website that could accommodate established customers, and reach new customers. As a business largely run by one person, we also needed to find an easy way for them to accurately keep track of inventory to make an online business feasible.


CONTACTLESS PICKUP

Our client also wanted to find a way to safely integrate contactless pickup into their physical space. Their location in a busy, crowded mall with limited signage introduced further complications to this goal.

Users

SOMALI AND MUSLIM COMMUNITIES

Yeeyo’s Boutique currently serves Minnesota’s Somali and Muslim communities from their location in South Minneapolis. Customers are familiar with the shop owner, and often ask for her recommendations while they are shopping. The small shop is cozy and friendly, and the owner’s family often help her during busy periods. Our test participants, sourced by our client, were excited to shop online and already were frequent users of grocery delivery apps and online shopping for everyday necessities and gifts

Process

DISCOVERY RESEARCH + Stakeholder Interview

We began the project with a stakeholder interview, and then conducted a competitive analysis of online stores selling similar goods. We examined them for a variety of e-commerce features, methods of communicating with customers, website layouts, and UI elements, and documented our findings in a spreadsheet.


FEATURE CARDS AND USER RESEARCH

Our next step was to create wireframes of features we wanted to include in the website. We used dot voting to determine a set of feature cards that we could fit into a one hour interview with test participants to help spark conversations around e-commerce experiences, and to get direct feedback on specific features. We uncovered several useful nuggets of information that contributed to how we determined which e-commerce platform would be the most useful to our client. For example, being able to use Apple or Google Pay was very important to our users, which completely eliminated one of our otherwise top contenders.


CONTEXTUAL INTERVIEW

I visited the physical shop in the 24 Mall with one of my team members. We discovered that the mall is very difficult for first time visitors to navigate, with minimal signs and busy, tight hallways. At the shop we got to speak with the shop owner and one of her children, and spoke with them about their experiences selling with customers and how they used the space. We gleaned more useful information that also informed our web platform selection. They had just begun using Square to process sales, and the platform we ultimately selected could update its online inventory to reflect sales made in the store using Square.


PERSONA, USER JOURNEY MAP, AND SCREEN FLOW

After interviewing users, customers and staff alike, we did a comparative analysis of e-commerce platforms and selected one that most closely aligned with our priorities - Square-inventory integration, multiple payment options, and a highly customizable builder. One of my teammates created a style sheet with colors, fonts, and a logo that reflected the tone of the shop. Meanwhile, I created a map of the site’s architecture reflecting the needs of our users and the capabilities of our chosen platform. I then used Figma to build out wireframes and create an interactive prototype. To fit our timeline, I prototyped the pages following a happy path from landing on the home page to adding an item to your cart. I included pages a new user would find useful, such as the about and contact pages.


CONTACTLESS PICKUP

My desk research on contactless pickup methods revealed that curbside pickup was the most popular option - unfortunately, this would not suit our client, as she was often the only one in the shop, and couldn’t guarantee that a second person would be present at any moment to leave the shop supervised. Our solution was to create a dedicated space for storing items for pickup, using a cart with a sign. We also knew finding the shop itself would likely be a problem for new users, so we created a design for an A-frame sign to sit outside the shop, and a simple map of the mall to include in the email telling users that their order is ready for pickup.


DESIGN EVALUATION AND ADJUSTMENTS

Our final stage of the project was to test the website prototype and contactless pickup storyboard with users. I ended up changing which template the website would use to accommodate user feedback about the product page layout and usability. Our final handoff to the client included a set of annotated wireframes from the updated prototype.
